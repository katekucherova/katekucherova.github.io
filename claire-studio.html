FP&A & Small Business Finance Case Study

Claire Studio — from 847 raw cash transactions to a full FP&A model.

A children's fitness, dance, and martial arts studio in Bataysk, Russia. I co-founded it, ran its finances end-to-end, and built this model from the studio's real operating records.

16-tab Excel model 5-page Power BI dashboard Nov 2019 – Jul 2022
About the Business

Claire — Studio of Physical & Aesthetic Development offered choreography, fitness, karate, and gymnastics across up to two rented halls, serving a peak of 53 students across five programs at its height in Spring 2021.

I co-founded the studio in November 2019 and held full ownership of its financial operations — serving as financial manager and payroll administrator throughout, along with day-to-day duties beyond finance to keep the studio lean. The studio operated from November 2019 through July 2022, at which point I emigrated to the United States and transferred the business to my co-founder.

Data Source & What Was Built

This project began with 847 real cash transactions from the studio's operating records — a messy mix of revenue, expenses, owner draws, capital contributions, and inventory purchases recorded in raw ledger format. I cleaned, classified, and restructured this data into a formula-driven FP&A model built entirely in Excel.

Key Business Insights

1. Rent was the dominant fixed cost — and the expansion made it bigger.

Rent accounted for $110,230 of total operating costs across all periods — the single largest expense category, exceeding payroll by nearly 3x. When the second hall was leased in early 2021, monthly rent increased from $2,800 to $4,675 — a 67% step-up in the largest fixed cost. The break-even analysis shows this expansion was not fully covered by direct expansion revenue until late 2021, but the total business revenue grew enough to absorb the higher cost base.

2. Choreography was the studio's core revenue driver throughout its life.

Choreography generated $141,000 across the full period — just over half of total revenue — and remained the largest single program even as karate launched in 2020 and hall rental income grew from 2021 onward. That concentration made pricing decisions on this one program, including a 25% price increase in September 2021, disproportionately important to the studio's overall trajectory.

3. COVID created a clean before/after view of the business.

April and May 2020 were excluded from the model due to full closure during COVID lockdowns. The reopening in June 2020 effectively resets the business — and the contrast is sharp. Pre-COVID revenue averaged around $4,050/month (Nov 2019–Mar 2020); post-reopening the studio rebuilt quickly, reaching $10,837 in September 2020. By 2022 the run-rate exceeded $13,000/month before the wind-down began.

4. The 2021 budget was favorable across nearly every line.

Actual 2021 revenue exceeded budget by $3,192 (2.7%), driven by strong choreography and karate enrollment. Expenses came in only $774 over budget despite adding a second hall — because advertising spend was $489 below plan and class supply costs were $297 below plan. NOI beat budget by $2,418. The one unfavorable revenue line was fitness, which recovered more slowly than planned.
